Product Philosophy

Before features,
there is intention.

The OGDEN Land Design Atlas is not a drawing application. It is a land intelligence system. Every feature, every AI output, every interface decision is grounded in six core values that make it distinct from any generic land-planning tool.

Stewardship over extraction
The tool asks "what does this land need?" before "what can this land produce?". Assessments lead with ecological opportunity, not yield potential.
Phased humility
Not everything is built at once. The Atlas rewards sequenced, realistic buildout — not fantasy master plans. Cost and complexity warnings are features, not obstacles.
Explainability over magic
Every AI output, every suggestion, every score must be traceable. If the Atlas can't explain why it made a recommendation, it should not make one.
Spiritual intentionality
Prayer spaces, quiet zones, dawn/dusk viewpoints, and contemplative circulation are first-class design elements — not optional add-ons.
Honesty about uncertainty
Data gaps are surfaced, not hidden. Confidence levels appear on analyses. The Atlas flags what it doesn't know.
Accessibility of wisdom
The tool should be usable by a landowner with no GIS training, a community elder evaluating a proposal, and a developer reading technical specs — simultaneously.

Data Sourcing Strategy

Every layer has a source.

The most common failure mode in land intelligence tools: features that look impressive but silently produce empty or wrong outputs because the underlying data doesn't exist. Every data type in the Atlas has a defined source tier — nothing is fabricated.

Tier Label Meaning Sources
Tier 1 Auto-populated Fetched automatically from public APIs on project creation. Always available.
Elevation (USGS 3DEP) · Parcel boundaries (county GIS) · Soils (SSURGO) · Hydrology (NHD) · Land cover (NLCD) · Climate normals (NOAA) · Floodplains (FEMA) · Wetlands (NWI)
Tier 2 User-provided Requires upload or manual entry. Unlocks advanced features when present.
Drone imagery · Orthomosaic · LiDAR · Survey files · Site photos · Soil test results · Water test data · Title map · Legal description
Tier 3 Derived / estimated Calculated from Tier 1 + 2 data. Confidence depends on input quality. Always labeled.
Slope · Aspect · Curvature · Watershed delineation · Viewshed · Microclimate estimates · Frost pocket probability · Runoff accumulation
AI Guardrails Framework

A thoughtful collaborator,
not an oracle.

AI is a powerful co-designer — and a liability if it speaks with false confidence. Every AI output in the Atlas must meet six non-negotiable standards before it reaches a user.

🔗
Sourced Reasoning
Every suggestion cites which data inputs it drew from. "Based on slope data (USGS 3DEP), soil drainage class (SSURGO), and your uploaded site plan…"
📊
Confidence Display
Every output carries a confidence level: High (well-sourced data, clear signal), Medium (some data gaps), Low (inference-heavy — verify on site).
💬
Uncertainty Language
Low-confidence outputs use hedged language: "This area may be prone to…" — never "This area is a frost pocket." The language matches the evidence.
🚩
Site Visit Flags
Any output with confidence below Medium that would influence a significant design decision triggers a "Verify on site" flag. This is a feature, not a disclaimer.
🚫
No Hallucinated Specifics
AI must not generate specific cost figures, yield projections, or regulatory determinations unless they are drawn from real data sources cited in the output.
✏️
Editable Outputs
All AI-generated text — summaries, design briefs, investor narratives — is editable by the user. AI output is a draft, not a decree.
Regulatory Intelligence

Where beautiful designs die — and where they survive.

A design that cannot be permitted is not a design — it is a dream. The Atlas surfaces regulatory reality early so it can be designed around, not discovered after construction.

🗺
Zoning + Setbacks
Current zoning designation, permitted use summary, and visual setback overlays — front, rear, side, and riparian — drawn on the parcel map.
🌊
Floodplains + Wetlands
FEMA flood zone designation with link to FIRM map. NWI wetlands with applicable buffer zones visualized on the design canvas.
📋
Permit Checklist
Auto-generated list of likely permits based on proposed uses. Regulatory risk score for the proposed design. Phase-specific permit dependency warnings.
💧
Water + Easements
Well and septic setback distances, water rights notes, utility and conservation easement overlays, agricultural exemption status.
🏠
Hospitality + Events
Zoning constraints on retreat and guest use flagged. Event permit requirements for public gatherings surfaced. Short-term rental notes integrated.
⚠️
Professional Review Flag
AI-triggered alert when proposed design raises complex regulatory questions that require a licensed engineer, attorney, or hydrologist to resolve.

Interface Design

The interface should feel like
standing on land.

Not operating software. Every UX decision is an opportunity to reinforce the values of the Atlas.

Contemplative Navigation
Camera movement is smooth and unhurried. Pan and zoom feel like walking, not clicking. No jarring transitions or frantic energy.
Presence Over Density
Default views show less, not more. The land should be visible. Data layers are surfaced intentionally, not piled on top of each other.
Honest Feedback
Errors and limitations stated plainly. No spinning loaders that hide failures. No empty charts. Honest empty states with clear next steps.
Progressive Depth
Simple mode shows what matters. Advanced mode reveals the Atlas's full capability. Users are never overwhelmed before they are ready.
Respect for Time
Every workflow should be completable in fewer steps than the user expects. The Atlas does more work so the user does less.
Quiet Confidence
Visual language is calm, grounded, and authoritative — not playful or trendy. Earth tones, natural textures, generous whitespace.
Accessibility as Hospitality
High contrast ratios, keyboard navigation, screen reader support, and legible typography are baseline requirements — not afterthoughts.
Offline + Low-Tech Ready
Projects cached for offline use. Field annotation without connectivity. View-only shareable links requiring no account. Print layouts for community meetings.
